INTRODUCTION
Background of the Study
To meet the demands of a fast-changing, information-driven society, educational systems must integrate technological tools that support flexible, individualized, and accessible learning (Atmaca-Aksoy, 2024). An LMS platform fosters an environment that cultivates engagement and learner achievement, enabling learners to register for classes, track their progress, and stay updated on course announcements and other relevant information. Fundamentally, an LMS behaves as a platform to distribute and oversee educational material (Bradley, 2020). Phan et al. (2022) state that systems like the LMS turn learning into a more active and interactive process, requiring more effort from students and increasing the retention of knowledge and the acquisition of new skills. Technology doesn’t simply provide convenience but a more efficient learning environment; this provides an opportunity for innovations like the integration of artificial intelligence.
        Chen et al. (2020) define artificial intelligence as the culmination of computers, computer-related technologies, machines, and information communication technology innovations and developments to give computers the ability to perform near-human or human-like functions. In line with the use of new technology, artificial intelligence has also been extensively utilized in the education sector. Artificial intelligence has a wide range of applications, including adaptive learning. Daugherty et al. (2022) state that adaptive learning provides students with the means to acquire information according to their individual needs and cognitive differences, thus facilitating the learning process of each individual. Despite its potential, adaptive learning has not been widely implemented in current LMS platforms.
        Current learning management systems provide the same services to all users regardless of their needs. Students learn according to their preferred methods, and a key move in making education flexible is to evaluate these preferences (Aldahwan & Alsaeed, 2020). Traditional one-size-fits-all educational approaches struggle to adequately cater to this individual variability, leading to suboptimal learning experiences and potentially hindering learners' overall performance (Das et al., 2023). Students in the same course do not all progress or feel satisfied in the same way because they start with different skill levels and learning habits. To support everyone, there needs to be a focus on strengthening the factors that drive improvement. By doing this, learners of all levels have a better chance to improve their skills, feel more confident about managing their learning, and be satisfied with their progress (Koenig & Guertler, 2021).
        Every learner possesses a unique set of strengths, weaknesses, and interests, which can significantly impact their academic performance and engagement in the learning process. It is of particular importance for example, in Massive Open Online Courses (MOOC), where the same course is followed by thousands of learners, all with different backgrounds, knowledge, and culture, making it impossible to provide efficient one-size-fits-all learning environments (Paquette et al., 2021).
        Educational experts often formulate policies that address a single or relatively small set of problems and then implement them, believing the solution they advocate will be efficient, complete, widely scalable, comprehensive, and easily implemented. Unfortunately, linear approaches are rarely sufficient to address complex issues as they are essentially incapable of generating viable solutions for a broader audience. In contrast, complex systems are characterized by “highly connected networks of semi-independent agents from which system-wide patterns emerge that can learn and adapt over time” (Gunawardena et al., 2023).
        The researchers' proposed solution is to develop a learning management system that utilizes artificial intelligence to adapt to learners' specific styles or preferences in studying to give them a more personalized learning experience. In the researchers’ study, the Felder-Silverman Learning Style Model (FSLSM) was applied to categorize students’ learning styles. This model defines each learner based on their distinct preferences across its dimensions. The system will be able to identify these learning styles using machine learning, specifically through the XGBoost algorithm.

Objectives of the Study
General Objective
This project aims to develop a learning management system that utilizes artificial intelligence to adapt to each learner’s unique study preferences, providing a more personalized learning experience. The goal is to make learning more personalized by allowing students to facilitate their learning using their preferred methods. The system will use the Felder-Silverman Learning Style Model (FSLSM) and apply the XGBoost algorithm to classify students’ learning styles.
Specific Objectives
* To design an adaptive learning management system that can transform course materials to students' specific learning styles to boost performance and engagement.
* To develop an adaptive learning system that tracks users behavior to find out each learner's preference by using XGBoost, the main component to determine the learning style of each student.  To transform learning material according to the user's learning preferences by utilizing Gemini AI, an artificial intelligence model used for providing transformed content within the adaptive learning management system.
* To evaluate the functionality of digital learning features through model evaluation metrics, performance of generative artificial intelligence, and testing the main functionalities of the learning management system.
* To evaluate the system by using Functionality, Usability, Reliability, Performance, and Supportability (FURPS) framework, and conducting a pre-test and post-test.
Scope and Limitations of the Study
Scope of the System
1. Functionalities: Core LMS functionalities such as course management, learning content management, and learning objects. 
2. Administrative Controls: Tools for managing users, courses, system settings, and monitoring overall platform health and usage.
3. Content Generation: The system is capable of taking learning materials and transforming them into other forms of material such as practice quizzes, questions and activities.
4. Supervised Machine Learning (Classification): The system applies supervised ML, specifically XGBoost, to classify students’ learning styles based on the Felder-Silverman Learning Style Model (FSLSM). This allows the platform to adapt course materials according to individual preferences.
5. Rule-Based Algorithm: To generate initial labels by mapping students’ behaviors, such as time spent on videos, replay counts, or forum participation, into preliminary categories. These labels serve only as a starting point for training the machine learning model, which then learns more accurate patterns from the data.
Limitations of the System
1. AI Model: The AI model includes a rate limit as it can vary by the license.
2. Assistance: The AI-generated content does not provide direct answers to the students’ assigned activities.
3. Learning Model: The Supervised ML (XGBoost) requires sufficient labeled training data; predictions may be inaccurate if the dataset is small or unbalanced.
4. Datasets: The acquired datasets used to train the machine learning algorithm may not be entirely compatible with the features present within the system.

Review of Related Literature and Studies
E-learning, Online Learning, and Distance Learning
Methods like face-to-face education have been the standard form of education. However, with the advancement of technology, online learning is becoming more popular. Although considered traditional, face-to-face and newer methods of learning, like online learning mechanisms, are indeed the learning methodologies that we can depend on in the 21st century. Many of our academic institutions rely on effective ways of teaching our learners and how we can develop a transformative learning innovation for their learning pleasure. Teachers must continually develop instructional strategies using innovative technology tools to ensure optimal development of students' critical thinking skills and overall academic performance in both asynchronous and synchronous learning environments. The effectiveness of online learning compared to face-to-face learning is a subject of much debate (Lee, 2024).
Culduz (2024) states that there are several key differences between online and traditional education. Firstly,  traditional training typically occurs in a specific place and at a particular time. Students are given a schedule of their weekly lessons, and the teacher expects them to attend the lesson at the specified time. This time dependency complicates the traditional approach to education for some students who have external commitments such as family or work. In these cases, the online education approach provides much-needed flexibility.
Additionally, the researchers provided a contrast between terms related to online education. E-learning refers to all educational activities carried out synchronously or asynchronously by individuals or groups working online or offline, through networked or independent computers and other electronic devices. Online education is a form of e-learning that involves learning experiences in synchronous or asynchronous environments utilizing various devices with internet access to deliver educational programs, courses, and certifications. In these environments, students can learn and interact with instructors and other students anywhere. Distance learning, on the other hand, is a form of education that features the physical separation of teachers and students during teaching and the use of various technologies to facilitate student-teacher and student-student communication. These forms of learning present a different set of affordances and challenges.
Virtual Learning Environment
Yadav et al. (1998) describe the virtual learning environment as a means to overcome the shortcomings of the traditional classroom environment and the distance learning method. Students and teachers do not have to meet at the same time and location. However, the students can retrieve course content at their own convenience and interact with the instructors according to their needs.
Kovtoniuk et al. (2022) emphasized that the important components of virtual learning environments are ensuring expediency of learning by maintaining the priority of learning over teaching; dominance of activity-based type of learning; organization of independent learning activities; increasing motivation for learning through the use of means of complex presentation and manipulation of audiovisual information; increasing the level of emotional perception of information and formation of abilities to implement various forms of independent activities for information processing.
Furthermore, the authors proposed that virtual learning environments are designed by using a modern learning management system (LMS), a web-based application that enables the administration of educational content in the distance learning process. The presence of a convenient learning management system will allow you to solve several problems that previously required a large number of disparate tools.

The symbolic school believes that AI is the operation of symbols, and the most primitive symbols correspond to the physical entities. Although the descriptions of AI are various, the core of AI is widely believed to be the research theories, methods, technologies, and applications for simulating, extending, and expanding human intelligence. Nowadays, the concept of AI has an increasingly profound impact on human life. Throughout the history of scientific and technological development, the emergence of any scientific and technological revolution is not only reflected in technology, but also changes in human social structure, moral constraints, laws, and education.

Popular techniques to minimize variance include cross-validation and bagging. However, boosting is commonly used for reducing bias.

Learning Style
Mestre (2012) describes learning styles as the various ways in which individuals prefer to receive and process information, influencing the instructional methods that teachers may employ. It encompasses the notion that recognizing and adapting to different learning styles can promote self-awareness, metacognition, and improved educational outcomes. While Kaur et al. (2023) state that learning styles refer to a set of personal traits that determine the effectiveness of teaching and learning methods for different individuals. It is an important factor in educational achievement and is positively correlated with academic performance in students.
Various Learning Style Models
Kolb’s learning cycle has four stages: concrete experience (CE), reflective observation (RO), abstract conceptualization (AC), and active experimentation (AE). Learners ideally move through each stage—starting with direct involvement in an experience (CE), reflecting on it from different perspectives (RO), forming ideas and theories (AC), and then testing those ideas through problem solving or decision-making (AE). Kolb’s experiential learning cycle is often applied only to short-term learning events, without considering its role in long-term learning that connects past and future experiences (Egan et al., 2023).
Honey and Mumford identified four learning styles: activist, reflector, theorist, and pragmatist. Activists learn best through trial and error, hands-on activities, and brainstorming. Theorists prefer to use logical frameworks and models to analyze and organize information. Pragmatists are eager to apply theories and techniques in real situations through systematic practice. Reflectors learn by carefully observing, reflecting on experiences, and considering how they contribute to outcomes. However, even though Honey & Mumford's learning styles focused on learning from experience, it did not guarantee whether the experience was effective learning, and the meaning of learning from experience by Honey & Mumford's learning styles was ambiguous (Ferdiani et al., 2021).
The VARK model is an educational framework for classifying learning preferences. Developed in 1987 by Fleming, it helps customize content to meet individual needs. It consists of four preferences: Visual (graphs, diagrams), Auditory (podcasts, conversations), Reading/Writing (reading, writing), and kinesthetic (hands-on activities). The VARK model is criticized for lacking strong validation of its four learning categories and for showing little evidence that matching teaching to student preferences improves learning outcomes (Melhem & Al-Zoubi, 2025).
Felder & Silverman Learning Style Model
Masegosa et al. (2024) define the Felder–Silverman Learning Styles Model (FSLSM), developed by Richard M. Felder and Linda K. Silverman (1988), which proposes that individuals have varying patterns of preferences and tendencies in learning and information processing. This model structures learning styles across four dimensions, each consisting of an opposing pair of categories that represent an individual’s preferred approach to learning. A person's preference within each dimension can range in intensity (strong, moderate, or mild). The FSLSM is widely regarded for its comprehensive approach to understanding educational experiences, with many researchers contending that learning styles play a crucial role in improving instructional design and learner engagement.
Carver et al. (1999) state that the Felder & Silverman Learning Style Model is the most appropriate for hypermedia courseware, such as a learning management system. Graf et al. (2007) state that this model is frequently used in research related to learning styles in advanced learning technologies and in technology-enhanced learning that is designed for traditional education.
The Four Dimensions of Felder & Silverman Learning Style Model
  

	Figure 10. Felder–Silverman Learning Style Dimensions.
	Source: https://tinyurl.com/3snp8t9n
	

Masegosa et al. (2024) expanded on the Felder–Silverman Learning Styles Model (FSLSM) by emphasizing its four key dimensions as seen in Figure 10.
Information processing: active or reflective learners. This dimension refers to how learners engage with new information. Active learners prefer to engage with the learning material through physical or interactive means. They learn best by doing, discussing, and actively participating in hands-on activities. They enjoy group work, experiments, and practical applications of knowledge. Active learners often thrive in collaborative environments where they can interact with their peers and instructors. Reflective learners, in contrast to active learners, prefer to process information internally. They are introspective and thoughtful, often taking their time to think through concepts before formulating their own understanding. Reflective learners benefit from quiet environments that allow for contemplation and introspection. They prefer individual study, writing, and self-reflection to solidify their understanding of new information, being fond of lectures and seminars.
Information perception: sensing or intuitive learners. This dimension relates to how learners perceive and process information. Sensing learners rely on their senses and prefer concrete, factual information. They appreciate practical examples, real-world applications, and hands-on experiences that allow them to engage with the subject matter in a tangible way. Sensing learners pay close attention to details, prefer step-by-step instructions, and may find memorization and repetition helpful in their learning process. Sensing learners tend to become distressed when faced with challenging tasks. They typically harbor concerns regarding the efficacy of academic practices and programs. Additionally, they dislike being assessed based on vague or embedded concepts and ideas. Intuitive learners are more interested in abstract and theoretical concepts. They enjoy exploring ideas, making connections between different concepts, and identifying underlying patterns and principles. Intuitive learners thrive in environments that encourage creativity, critical thinking, and conceptual understanding. They often seek out the bigger picture and are comfortable with ambiguity and uncertainty. Activities involving computation, rote learning, and conventional practices are unattractive to them. 
Information input: visual or verbal learners. This dimension describes the preferred mode of input for learners. Visual learners learn best through visual aids and representations. They prefer information presented in the form of diagrams, charts, graphs, and visual illustrations. Visual learners exhibit a higher capacity for observation, so they benefit from seeing relationships, spatial arrangements, and visual patterns. They may use color coding, mind maps, or visual organizers to enhance their understanding and retention of information. Verbal learners prefer to learn through written and spoken words. They excel in activities such as reading, writing, listening to lectures, and engaging in discussions. Verbal learners are skilled at understanding and remembering information when it is presented through words, explanations, and verbal instructions. They may benefit from reading textbooks, taking notes, and discussing concepts with others.
Information understanding: sequential or global learners. This dimension reflects the learners’ approach to organizing and processing information. Sequential learners prefer learning in a linear and step-by-step manner. They thrive when presented with a logical progression of information and appreciate clear and structured instructions. Sequential learners prefer to understand each concept thoroughly before moving on to the next. They often excel in subjects that involve logical reasoning, problem-solving, and following well-defined processes. Global learners have a holistic approach to learning and prefer to see the big picture. They can quickly grasp overarching concepts and connections without necessarily needing detailed step-by-step instructions. Global learners enjoy synthesizing information, making associations, and understanding the broader context. They may struggle with tasks that require a linear approach or intricate, sequential details.

Initial Dataset
Bittner et al. (2023) conducted an eye-tracking study with 117 individuals. Specific patterns of the participants’ eye tracking behaviour are attributed to their learning styles, as determined by the validated Index of Learning Styles (ILS) questionnaire, applying more than just the metrics commonly used in eye tracking research. Hypothesis-driven analysis, as well as Machine Learning (ML) methods, are used to supplement these investigations, allowing for more in-depth analytics. By combining eye tracking data with ML algorithms, an accurate and insightful understanding of students’ individual learning paths can be achieved, which could ultimately contribute to better educational results and decreased dropout rates.
In this experiment, calibration of the eye tracker is performed on each participant to ensure an appropriate experimental setup. As soon as successful calibration is achieved, the eye tracking experiment begins with introductions before each task. The presentation of the stimuli is not restricted in time. There is no need for prior knowledge of the information contained in the stimuli. As this experiment is based on the within-subject design, each stimulus is exposed to each subject. Following completion of the experiment, participants anonymously complete the ILS questionnaire. To avoid any influence on the experiment, it is important that the participants complete the questionnaire after the study and not beforehand. The questionnaire aids triangulation and provides valuable target variables for classification, enabling validation and testing of the study results using information convergence from multiple sources. Finally, the findings from the eye movement research are utilized to predict the learning styles as determined by each student’s responses to the ILS questionnaire.
The results stated that the machine learning model would correctly classify at least 80% of the participants' learning style attributes for each of the four FSLSM dimensions, which made it a plausible data source.

* E-learning: All educational activities, whether synchronous or asynchronous, conducted by individuals or groups using networked or independent computers and other electronic devices, for the purpose of delivering or engaging with educational content.
* Online Learning: A specific form of e-learning that involves educational experiences utilizing various devices with internet access to deliver programs, courses, and certifications in synchronous or asynchronous environments, enabling students to learn and interact with instructors and peers from any location.
* Distance Learning: An educational model characterized by the physical separation of teachers and students during instruction, using various technologies to facilitate communication and content delivery.
* Virtual Learning Environment (VLE): A digital platform, typically implemented using a modern Learning Management System (LMS), designed to overcome the constraints of traditional classrooms and distance learning by allowing students to retrieve course content and interact with instructors at their own convenience and location.
* Learning Management System (LMS): "A web-based application (e.g., Moodle) that serves as the administrative and delivery platform for educational content in distance learning. 
* Artificial Intelligence (AI): The research theories, methods, technologies, and applications for simulating, extending, and expanding human intelligence in machines. In an educational context, it is operationally defined by its integrated use in technologies like chatbots, automated grading systems, and personalized tutoring to provide adaptive feedback and profile students.
* Generative AI: A category of AI (e.g., ChatGPT) used by students and educators to generate new content, such as simple explanations, concise summaries, self-assessment questions, and quizzes/assignments, or to provide streamlined learning and feedback tools.
* Machine Learning (ML): An aspect of AI that focuses on building analytical models through automation that allow machines to adapt and predict based on past results. Operationally utilized through algorithms like Decision Trees, Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting for tasks such as predicting student outcomes and customizing learning experiences.
* Decision Tree: A specific tree-like structure algorithm used for classification and regression tasks. Operationally defined by its structure where nodes represent a test on an attribute and leaf nodes contain the final prediction.
* Ensemble Learning/Methods: A machine learning technique that integrates several different individual models (weak learners) to combine their predictions and yield a single, superior forecast (e.g., Random Forest, Boosting).
* Random Forest: An ensemble learning method that constructs numerous decision trees during training, each built on a randomly sampled subset of data. The final prediction is made by aggregating the predictions of all individual trees.
* Boosting: A powerful ensemble learning method in machine learning that aims to convert a set of weak predictive models (called "weak learners”) into a single, highly accurate model (a "strong learner").
* XGBoost (eXtreme Gradient Boosting): A gradient boosting framework and decision-tree-based ensemble method that sequentially builds classification or regression trees, where each subsequent model is trained to correct the residuals/mistakes of the previous one"
* GridSearchCV: A hyperparameter tuning method that utilizes an exhaustive grid search over a predefined hyper-parameter space, evaluating each combination's performance through K-fold cross-validation.
* K-Fold Cross-Validation (CV): A technique for assessing the generalizability of a machine learning model by partitioning the dataset into k equal subsets (folds). The model is trained on k−1 folds and tested on the remaining fold, repeating the process k times, with the final performance being the average across all trials.
* Model Evaluation Metrics: Statistical measures used to quantify the performance of a machine learning model (e.g., Random Forest, Neural Network) against a test dataset. Key metrics include Accuracy, Precision, Recall, and F1 Score"
* Accuracy: Evaluates how well an algorithm assigns individuals to the correct category.
* Precision: The percentage of samples predicted to be “positive” that are actually “positive”
* Recall: Refers to the percentage of “positive” samples that were correctly labelled as positive
* F1 Score: The harmonic mean of these precision and recall metrics. Therefore, a high F1 score requires both a high precision and a high recall.
* Feature Engineering: The practice of constructing suitable, new features from existing raw data/features by applying transformation functions (e.g., arithmetic, aggregate operators) to improve the predictive performance of machine learning models in identifying student success or learning styles.
* Felder & Silverman Learning Style Model (FSLSM): "An educational framework that categorizes individual learning preferences and tendencies across four dimensions, each with an opposing pair of categories: Active/Reflective, Sensing/Intuitive, Visual/Verbal, and Sequential/Global."
* Adaptive/Personalized Learning (AL): An educational approach where the learning experience (e.g., resources, activities, instructional strategy) is tailored to the individual student's needs and preferences to enhance engagement and performance.

This ensures that only users who successfully complete this required two-factor verification process are granted access to the Intelevo System.

Operations and Testing Procedures
The operation and testing phase of a Learning Management System (LMS) is a critical quality assurance process designed to bridge the gap between initial development and real-world deployment. By evaluating the software, researchers and developers can guarantee that the platform is effective for educational delivery.
Before testing is conducted, the environment is prepared where all components are synchronized for full functionality. To mitigate the risk of user error skewing the results, participants were guided through a preliminary demonstration and provided with specific instructional sets. This was critical in the user experience, ensuring that the interactions made with the system are representative of actual operational conditions, ultimately resulting in a validated evaluation of the system’s educational efficacy.
The testing itself was split to address both the technical and the qualitative aspects of the platform. The functional analysis verified that the underlying architecture—such as database retrieval and module transitions—remained stable. Simultaneously, the efficacy testing evaluated the platform’s success as an educational tool, focusing on how a user could navigate the learning materials and achieve learning objectives.
Pretest and Posttest
Pre-testing, also known as baseline testing, serves as the initial assessment conducted before the implementation of an intervention or program. It establishes a foundational benchmark, allowing researchers to discern the starting point of participants or variables under study. By providing a baseline against which subsequent changes can be measured, pre-testing offers invaluable context for interpreting the results of interventions.
On the other hand, post-testing involves assessment conducted after the intervention has been implemented. Its primary objective is to gauge the changes or outcomes resulting from the intervention compared to the baseline established during pre-testing. Post-testing enables researchers to evaluate the effectiveness of interventions, monitor participants' progress over time, and make informed decisions based on evidence.
Together, pre and post-testing form a dynamic framework for research and evaluation, offering insights into the effectiveness of interventions and contributing to evidence-based decision-making. In this context, the pretest and posttest design will be used for the following:
Evaluating the efficacy of Generative AI in enhancing student comprehension by tailoring instructional materials to individual learning preferences. By investigating the system's capacity to dynamically adapt content delivery to accommodate diverse learning styles.
Validating the precision of its automated detection engine against the established Index of Learning Styles (ILS) psychometric standard.
1. Define Learning Objectives: Establish and communicate explicit pedagogical goals for all instructional materials.
2. Develop Assessment: Design validated testing tools, such as multiple-choice and Likert-scale assessments, aligned with the defined objectives.
3. Conduct Pretest: Execute a pretest to determine the learners' initial knowledge state prior to platform exposure.
4. Deployment: Implement the AI-driven learning management system, leveraging Machine Learning to deliver personalized content.
5. Conduct Posttest: Conduct a post-intervention test to quantify knowledge acquisition and retention.

1. Functional Identification: Identify the various functions that the software needs to execute.

Project Description
Intellevo is a learning management system designed to assist learners in finding their preferred learning styles based on the Felder-Silverman Learning Style Model through the use of machine learning and allows learners to transform content based on those learning preferences by utilizing generative artificial intelligence. To ensure that the application runs regardless of where it is deployed, the application will be packaged using Docker. The testing procedure involves testing the system by its ability to adapt to the users preferences and the ability to transform content uploaded to the learning management system.

Project Structure
        The overall structure of the Intelevo system integrates artificial intelligence, machine learning, and web-based technologies into a unified adaptive learning platform. The system is designed to personalize educational content by analyzing student interaction data dynamically adjusting learning materials according to individual learning styles. 
        The system begins with user interaction through a web-based interface where students and instructors access the platform. As users engage with lessons, quizzes, and activities, the system continuously collects behavioral data such as navigation patterns, response time, and module, which classifies learners based on The Felder-Silverman Learning Style Model (FSLSM).
        The processed data is then utilized by the content personalization engine, which transforms learning materials into adaptive formats such as textual explanations, visual diagrams, audio narration, and interactive exercises. The system operates entirely online, a centralized web platform.
        The Intelevo interface is designed to provide users with a comprehensive and structured learning environment, including course management, adaptive content delivery, and performance monitoring.
